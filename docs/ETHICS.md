# Research Ethics

## Purpose

This document explains the ethical principles guiding the Open Negative Results Registry.

## Core principles

### Transparency

Research outcomes should be recorded honestly, even when they are null, negative, or less publishable than expected.

### Respect for participants

When human subjects contribute time, data, or risk to research, their contribution should not disappear from the scientific record without good reason.

### Non-maleficence

Hiding failed or harmful interventions can contribute to repeated errors, wasted resources, and avoidable harm in future studies or clinical settings.

### Integrity

The repository should prioritize accuracy, traceability, and clarity over sensationalism or selective reporting.

### Privacy and confidentiality

No submission should include protected health information, personal identifiers, or material that violates institutional, legal, or ethical restrictions.

## Inclusion criteria

Entries should be considered for inclusion when they:

- Document a genuine negative, null, or inconclusive result.
- Are described clearly enough to be understood and reviewed.
- Include enough metadata to support future reuse or comparison.
- Do not expose private or restricted data.

## Exclusion criteria

Entries should be excluded or revised if they:

- Contain personal identifiers.
- Misrepresent a positive finding as negative.
- Lack basic methodological context.
- Present speculative claims without evidence.
- Breach ethical, legal, or institutional obligations.

## Curatorial responsibility

Curators should evaluate submissions in good faith, applying the same standards of accuracy and relevance to every entry. The goal is not to reward or punish outcomes, but to preserve useful scientific evidence responsibly.

## Final note

Negative findings are not failures of ethics. They become an ethical problem only when they are hidden, distorted, or lost without justification.
