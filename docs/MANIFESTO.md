## 1. Preamble

Science is often described as humanity’s most reliable method for discovering truth. It is praised for its rigor, its self-correcting mechanisms, and its ability to turn uncertainty into knowledge. Yet these ideals depend on a deeper condition that is often neglected: the integrity of the scientific archive. If the archive is selective, then the knowledge built upon it will also be selective. If the record privileges success and suppresses failure, then science may remain productive while still becoming incomplete.

The Open Negative Results Registry arises from this recognition. It is not merely a repository. It is an argument about what science must remember in order to remain honest. It proposes that negative results, null findings, failed hypotheses, terminated studies, and inconclusive outcomes are not peripheral traces of research. They are central evidence, and they deserve a durable place in the epistemic record.

This manifesto is therefore both a declaration and a correction. It declares a commitment to epistemic symmetry. It corrects the assumption that only favorable findings are worthy of preservation.

## 2. Foundational Thesis

The foundational thesis of this project is simple: scientific knowledge is incomplete when its record excludes what did not work.

This principle has profound implications. The omission of negative data distorts the literature, biases evidence synthesis, and encourages the repetition of already failed ideas. It also creates a false impression of consensus by allowing the visible record to overrepresent success. In such a system, publication becomes a filter not merely for quality, but for narrative appeal.

The registry resists that distortion. It affirms that science is not only a search for what confirms expectation, but also a disciplined process of eliminating error, narrowing uncertainty, and learning from non-confirmation. Negative results are not a failure of science. They are an achievement of methodological honesty.

## 3. The Problem of Selective Publication

Modern research culture is shaped by incentives that reward visibility. Positive results are more likely to be published, cited, disseminated, and celebrated. Negative results, by contrast, are often left unpublished or minimally documented. This asymmetry is not harmless. It alters the structure of the scientific record itself.

When selective publication becomes normalized, the literature no longer reflects the full range of empirical outcomes. It becomes a curated archive of preferred findings. This has consequences for medicine, psychology, biology, pharmacology, epidemiology, and many other fields. Researchers may unknowingly pursue already exhausted hypotheses. Clinicians may rely on incomplete evidence. Funders may invest in projects that appear promising only because contrary evidence is invisible.

This is not simply a technical problem of indexing or data management. It is a structural epistemic problem. The literature becomes less trustworthy not because scientists cease to produce data, but because the system fails to preserve the data that challenge dominant expectations.

## 4. The Epistemic Status of Negative Results

Negative results possess genuine epistemic value. They refine theories, constrain speculation, and expose the boundaries of current understanding. A null finding can reveal that an intervention is ineffective under certain conditions, that a hypothesis is too weak to survive testing, or that a sample, method, or theoretical framework requires revision.

To dismiss such findings as unimportant is to misunderstand the logic of inquiry. Science advances not only by affirmation, but by differentiation. Every unsuccessful test improves the precision of what remains plausible. Every refutation removes a layer of confusion. Every null result reduces the space of error.

In this sense, negative results are not secondary to positive ones. They are part of the same epistemic process. They do not merely accompany discovery; they enable discovery by marking the paths that should not be repeated.

## 5. The Archival Duty

To archive failure is to honor the full trajectory of scientific inquiry. Negative results, aborted protocols, terminated studies, and disproven assumptions are not peripheral artifacts; they are constitutive elements of the research process. A system that preserves only success produces not a record of science, but a mythology of inevitability.

The archival imperative extends beyond successful findings to the preservation of failure itself. When unsuccessful hypotheses, null outcomes, and terminated studies are left undocumented, science loses not only data but memory: the capacity to learn from what did not work, to avoid redundant experimentation, and to preserve the genealogy of inquiry. Archiving failures is therefore not an administrative luxury but an epistemic obligation, for every unrecorded dead end becomes an invitation for others to repeat the same costly mistake in silence.

This duty applies equally to large institutions and to independent researchers. The scale of the study does not determine the importance of the lesson. A failed pilot study, an abandoned intervention, or a non-confirmatory replication may each contain information that is indispensable to future work.

## 6. Silence as a Form of Structural Loss

Silence in research is rarely empty. More often, it is structured by institutional incentives, editorial gatekeeping, professional competition, and the cultural assumption that only positive findings deserve visibility. This produces an archival economy in which some forms of evidence are systematically elevated while others disappear.

The result is not neutrality. The result is loss. A study that is not published may still be methodologically sound. A null result that remains hidden may still be scientifically meaningful. A terminated trial that is never indexed may still reveal important limits of feasibility, safety, or efficacy. To ignore these materials is to confuse absence of visibility with absence of value.

Silence, in this sense, is a form of epistemic injury. It removes evidence from circulation without removing its relevance. It harms the continuity of scientific memory by preventing future researchers from seeing where knowledge has already reached its limits.

## 7. Publication Bias and Its Consequences

Publication bias is one of the most persistent distortions in the modern research environment. It creates a literature disproportionately populated by successful, dramatic, or statistically significant outcomes. Over time, this can generate a false picture of how often hypotheses succeed and how strong certain effects truly are.

The consequences are serious. Meta-analyses may overestimate effect sizes. Replication crises may deepen. Clinical guidelines may be influenced by a partial evidence base. Pharmaceutical and biomedical research may expend enormous resources on pathways already shown to be weak or unpromising. The social cost of this bias is not trivial; it accumulates through wasted time, misallocated funding, and avoidable error.

A registry of negative results does not eliminate publication bias on its own, but it helps counteract it by making the hidden part of the record visible. Transparency is not the complete solution, but it is an essential precondition for repair.

## 8. The Ethics of Scientific Memory

Scientific memory is not merely technical storage. It is ethical preservation. When participants consent to research, they do so in the expectation that their contribution will expand public knowledge. If the resulting data are selectively omitted because they are not exciting enough, the moral contract of research becomes weaker.

This is especially true in medical and clinical settings. Patients and volunteers are exposed to risk, inconvenience, and uncertainty in the service of collective understanding. That understanding must not be reduced to a narrow subset of publishable successes. A research culture that erases negative results risks betraying the trust on which it depends.

The ethics of memory therefore require that science remember more than its triumphs. It must remember its limitations, reversals, and failures as well. Only then can it claim to honor those who made research possible.

## 9. Why Failure Must Be Archived

Failure must be archived because failure is informative. It tells us what not to pursue, what not to assume, and what not to repeat. A single unpublished null result may prevent multiple future teams from investing time, funding, and effort into an unproductive direction.

Archiving failure also preserves methodological transparency. It allows future researchers to assess whether a line of inquiry has already been tested under comparable conditions. It strengthens literature reviews. It improves grant planning. It supports more accurate meta-analytic models. It creates a fuller map of scientific exploration.

Without this archive, the same dead ends are rediscovered repeatedly, but never formally acknowledged as dead ends. That is not efficiency. That is costly oblivion. The registry exists to convert such oblivion into usable memory.

## 10. Reproducibility, Replication, and the Value of Non-Confirmation

Replicability is one of the cornerstones of scientific credibility, but replication cannot function properly if negative findings are underreported. Non-confirmation is not a defect in the process. It is part of the process. It signals that a result should be interpreted cautiously, that an effect may be context-dependent, or that the original claim may not generalize.

A robust scientific culture must therefore treat failed replications and null replications not as embarrassments, but as evidence. The inability to reproduce a result is itself a result. It carries implications for theory, method, and interpretation. To hide it is to weaken the foundations of self-correction.

The registry is designed to support this broader culture of reproducibility by giving non-confirmatory outcomes a durable and searchable home.

## 11. The Registry as Epistemic Infrastructure

The Open Negative Results Registry is not simply a storage system. It is an epistemic infrastructure: a structure through which invisible evidence becomes durable, searchable, and usable. By organizing negative findings in structured form, the registry helps researchers retrieve prior attempts, compare outcomes, and avoid redundant work.

This infrastructure is particularly important in the age of computational science. Machine learning systems, evidence synthesis tools, and automated review pipelines all depend on the quality and balance of the data they ingest. If the archive is biased toward positive findings, then the models built from it will inherit that bias. Better infrastructure therefore means better intelligence, better inference, and better decisions.

The registry also creates space for curation, traceability, and versioned preservation. It can serve as a future-facing platform for a more transparent scientific ecosystem.

## 12. Scientific Symmetry and Public Trust

Scientific symmetry means that the record should not systematically favor one kind of outcome over another. This does not imply that all findings are equal in every respect. It means that all serious findings deserve the possibility of being preserved and examined.

Symmetry matters because public trust depends on honesty of record. If science is presented as a field in which only success survives, then the public will eventually discover that the apparent certainty was built on omission. Trust declines when people realize that the archive was curated to flatter the institution rather than to represent reality.

A balanced archive is therefore not merely an academic ideal. It is a public necessity.

## 13. A Commitment to the Common Good

This project is guided by a commitment to the common good. It is not motivated by a desire to valorize failure for its own sake. It is motivated by the belief that society benefits when evidence is preserved honestly and comprehensively.

Negative results reduce waste. They improve future research planning. They help protect patients. They support better education. They make science more efficient and more truthful. By preserving what did not work, we increase the likelihood that what does work will be found sooner and used more responsibly.

This registry is therefore an instrument of public value. It helps transform private disappointment into collective knowledge.

## 14. Principles of Governance and Curation

A repository of this kind must be governed carefully. It must maintain standards for inclusion, respect privacy and confidentiality, and distinguish between rigorous negative findings and unsupported speculation. Its curation should be transparent, consistent, and responsible.

The following principles are essential:

- Preserve only evidence that is scientifically relevant and ethically shareable.
- Require sufficient metadata for interpretation and reuse.
- Screen for privacy violations, duplication, and methodological ambiguity.
- Treat contributors with respect regardless of outcome.
- Maintain openness without sacrificing quality.

Curation is not censorship. It is stewardship. A serious registry must protect both integrity and accessibility.

## 15. Final Declaration

This manifesto affirms that science becomes more trustworthy when it is willing to remember what it would rather forget.

The Open Negative Results Registry exists to preserve that memory. It exists to defend the value of non-confirmation, to archive the logic of failure, and to protect future inquiry from the cost of repeated silence. It is a declaration that the full history of research matters, not only the parts that are easy to publish.

To archive failure is to defend truth.
To preserve the negative is to protect the future of science.
To make silence visible is to restore epistemic justice.
