# Custodians of Absence: An Epistemic and Institutional Account of the Dark Data Medicine Registry

*A dissertation-style thesis on the conceptual foundations of `open-negative-results-registry`*

---

## Abstract

Biomedical science keeps a strange kind of ledger. It records what worked and, far less reliably, what did not. This asymmetry is not accidental; it is produced, sustained, and reproduced by the institutional machinery that decides what counts as worth writing down. This thesis examines the Dark Data Medicine registry as a deliberate intervention into that machinery — not as a technical artifact to be described, but as a philosophical position to be argued for. I situate the registry within a longer conversation about testimony, silence, and epistemic injustice in the sciences, drawing on the sociology of scientific knowledge, the philosophy of evidence, and the specific empirical history of the "file drawer problem." I argue that the registry's technical choices — its schema, its curation model, its refusal of narrative form — are not incidental engineering decisions but answers to a question philosophy of science has asked for decades without institutional follow-through: what happens to knowledge that fails to confirm, and who is responsible for its disappearance? The thesis closes by examining what the registry cannot do, because a project that claims no limits has usually misunderstood its own ambitions.

## 1. Introduction

Every science has an unwritten archive. Somewhere between the laboratory notebook and the published paper there is a filter, and what does not pass through it is not preserved so much as it simply stops existing in any form another researcher could later find. This is true of physics, of psychology, of economics. It is acutely true of biomedicine, where the filter operates on human subjects, animal models, and public money, and where its consequences are not merely intellectual.

The phrase most commonly used for what falls through this filter — "dark data" — borrows its metaphor from astronomy, where dark matter is inferred from its gravitational effects rather than observed directly. The analogy is imperfect but instructive. We know dark data exists the same way astronomers know dark matter exists: not by seeing it, but by noticing that the visible universe of published results does not behave the way it should if it were the whole story. Meta-analyses correct for it with statistical machinery — funnel plots, trim-and-fill methods, PET-PEESE regressions — that essentially estimate the shape of an absence. These are sophisticated tools for measuring a hole. They do not fill it.

This thesis is not a technical description of the Dark Data Medicine registry, which is documented exhaustively elsewhere in its own repository, chapter by chapter, script by script. It is instead an argument about what the registry *is*, philosophically, when stripped of its JSON schemas and its GitHub Actions workflows. My claim is that the registry constitutes a specific, contestable answer to a question that has mostly been treated as rhetorical: if negative results are epistemically equivalent to positive ones, why does no institution treat them that way in practice? The registry's answer is not a policy paper. It is an infrastructure — small, imperfect, and already running — built on the premise that the question deserves an answer made of code and governance documents rather than another position paper calling for change that never arrives.

I want to be honest, at the outset, about the register in which this thesis is written. It borrows the form of a doctoral chapter because that form disciplines an argument: it asks for a genealogy, a theoretical framework, an account of mechanism, and a reckoning with limits. It does not claim the institutional standing of a dissertation defended before a committee. What it claims is that the registry deserves to be thought about at that level of seriousness, and that thinking about it seriously means asking harder questions of it than a product description would.

## 2. The Problem of Missing Evidence

The empirical study of missing evidence in science has a name and a date. Robert Rosenthal gave it both in 1979, in a short paper in *Psychological Bulletin* titled "The file drawer problem and tolerance for null results." Rosenthal's insight was not that negative results go unpublished — everyone in a laboratory already knew that — but that this fact could be made to bite back against the published literature itself. He proposed a way of calculating how many unpublished null results would have to exist, sitting in filing cabinets, to nullify a given published finding. The calculation was a kind of dare: if the number needed to overturn a well-established effect is implausibly large, we can have some confidence the effect is real; if it is small, we should worry.

What Rosenthal formalized had already been observed, less quantitatively, throughout the twentieth century. Statisticians had long noted that published effect sizes tended to be larger, cleaner, and more improbable than pure chance would predict, given how many studies must have been run to produce them. The mechanism did not require dishonesty. It required only that journals prefer to publish surprising, positive, statistically significant findings, and that researchers, aware of this preference, direct their effort accordingly — a feedback loop later formalized under names like publication bias, the winner's curse, and, in its most damning articulation, John Ioannidis's 2005 argument that most published research findings are false, precisely because the selection pressure toward significance outpaces the base rate of true effects in most fields where hypotheses are cheap to generate and expensive to falsify.

Ioannidis's paper is worth pausing on, because it is frequently cited and rarely followed to its actual conclusion. The claim is not that scientists are careless or dishonest. It is a probabilistic argument: given a field's typical effect sizes, sample sizes, and the number of hypotheses tested before one is published, the posterior probability that a published positive finding is true can fall below fifty percent even when every individual step in producing it was methodologically sound. The problem is structural, not personal, which is exactly why an individual researcher's integrity cannot fix it. Only a change in what the field collectively chooses to preserve can.

Biomedicine's version of this problem carries a distinctive severity, because its negative results are not merely statistically informative — they are, in a specific and literal sense, safety information. A trial that finds no benefit, or finds harm, tells the next investigator not to repeat the exposure. When that trial disappears from the record, the next investigator does not inherit caution; they inherit ignorance dressed as a blank slate, and sometimes a research subject inherits the consequences. The high-dose chemotherapy and autologous bone marrow transplantation episode in breast cancer treatment, pursued through the 1980s and into the 1990s on the strength of early uncontrolled studies before larger randomized trials found no survival benefit, is often cited for exactly this reason: not because anyone lied, but because the negative signal that should have arrived earlier arrived too late and too quietly to change what was already standard practice.

What Rosenthal's file-drawer metaphor gets slightly wrong, for present purposes, is its implication of intentional concealment. A drawer is something you close on purpose. Most dark data is not hidden; it is simply never transcribed into a form durable enough to survive the researcher who generated it. A negative finding usually dies not because someone suppresses it but because no one gets around to writing it up, and the incentive structure of academic careers gives no one a reason to. This is a distinction with real consequences for what kind of intervention can address it. A policy against suppression addresses malice. An infrastructure problem requires infrastructure.

## 3. Testimony, Silence, and the Social Architecture of Belief

Philosophy of science spent much of the twentieth century asking what makes a claim scientific, and comparatively little time asking what makes a claim *rememberable*. Karl Popper's falsificationism gave negative results a starring theoretical role — a single disconfirming instance, on his account, can topple a universal claim that a thousand confirming instances could never fully secure — and yet the institutions Popper described his philosophy within never built the machinery to actually collect disconfirming instances at scale. The asymmetry is almost embarrassing in retrospect: a philosophy that assigns negative evidence the decisive logical role, operating inside a publication system that assigns it the least career value of any result a laboratory can produce.

Michael Polanyi's account of tacit knowledge offers a different angle on the same gap. Polanyi argued that a great deal of what scientists know is never fully explicit — it lives in trained judgment, in the feel of an experiment, in things a researcher would recognize but could not easily write down. Negative results sit in an odd relationship to this idea. They are, in principle, exactly the kind of finding that *should* be explicit — a clean, sayable fact: this did not work — and yet they behave, institutionally, like tacit knowledge anyway, passed along informally between labs that happen to know each other, or between a principal investigator and their own students, but rarely committed to a form a stranger could retrieve. The knowledge is propositional in content and tacit in circulation. That mismatch is, in a fairly direct sense, the exact problem this registry is built to correct.

Bruno Latour and Steve Woolgar's ethnographic account of laboratory life, and the broader sociology of scientific knowledge that followed it, pushed further: what counts as a fact is not simply discovered but constructed through a social process of inscription, circulation, and citation. A finding that is never inscribed into a durable, circulable form has not merely failed to become well-known. In an important sense, it has failed to become a fact at all, because factuality, on this account, is partly constituted by the network of documents and citations that stabilize a claim against doubt. A negative result trapped in a lab notebook is, from this vantage, not a quiet fact. It is a fact that never finished being made.

Helen Longino's social epistemology adds a normative dimension that the descriptive sociology leaves open. Longino argued that objectivity in science is not a property of individual minds working in isolation but an achievement of a community structured to allow genuine criticism — a community with, in her terms, publicly recognized avenues for the criticism of evidence, reasoning, and background assumptions, and with some form of uptake, where that criticism can actually change the shared body of belief. A scientific community that has no mechanism for a negative result to enter public circulation has, on Longino's account, a structural objectivity deficit — not because any individual scientist is biased, but because the community lacks a channel through which disconfirming evidence can do its work.

There is a term from a different corner of philosophy — Miranda Fricker's epistemic injustice — that was developed to describe how certain speakers are systematically not believed or not heard, owing to prejudice about who counts as a credible source. I want to use it cautiously here, because Fricker's original cases involve identity-based prejudice against persons, and a negative clinical trial is not a person. But there is a structural cousin of the phenomenon worth naming: a kind of injustice done not to a speaker but to a *finding*, which is systematically denied uptake not because it is false but because of what kind of finding it is. Call it, provisionally, epistemic disposability — the condition of a true claim that the prevailing incentive structure treats as not worth keeping. Negative results in biomedicine are, in this specific and narrow sense, epistemically disposable, and a registry built to catch them before they are discarded is, whether or not its authors would use this language, an attempt at repair.

## 4. Architecture as Argument

A registry is not a neutral container. Every field it asks a contributor to fill in, and every field it declines to ask for, encodes a claim about what matters. The Dark Data Medicine schema is short by design — roughly ten required fields — and that brevity is itself the first argument the system makes: that the primary obstacle to preserving a negative result has never been a shortage of tools for describing it in exhaustive detail, but the size of the gap between having a finding and having anywhere to put it. A researcher who has just watched a two-year study fail to reach significance is not in the mood to fill out a form modeled on a full manuscript. The registry's minimalism is a wager that most dark data is lost at the very first step — the decision of whether disclosure is worth any effort at all — and that the only design response with any chance of working is to make that first step nearly frictionless.

This wager has a cost, and the system's architects have been explicit about paying it rather than pretending it away. A ten-field schema cannot carry statistical detail rich enough to feed directly into a formal meta-analysis; it cannot encode confidence intervals, Bayes factors, or a structured account of risk of bias. The registry's own roadmap acknowledges this and proposes a second, optional layer of fields for contributors able to supply them, rather than raising the floor for everyone and thereby excluding the independent researcher or the early-career clinician who is precisely the population most likely to be sitting on an undocumented negative result. This is a genuine trade-off, not a rhetorical one, and it is worth stating plainly rather than resolving it with a sentence that pretends both goals were achieved at once. The system chose reach over depth, at the current stage of its existence, and reach is the harder thing to get later if you do not secure it first.

The second architectural argument concerns structure over narrative. Free text is legible to a human reader and nearly invisible to a query. A negative finding described only in prose — "we tried X against Y and it did not work" — is discoverable only by someone who happens to read that particular sentence. Encoded as structured data, against a controlled vocabulary of disease domains, intervention types, and institutional categories, the same finding becomes something a stranger on another continent can find by asking the dataset a question rather than by reading it end to end. This is not merely a convenience. It is the difference between a fact that exists and a fact that can be found, which — per the Latour and Woolgar point above — may be closer to the difference between a fact that exists and a fact that does not.

The third argument is the curation requirement itself, and it deserves more scrutiny than the other two, because it is the one place the registry's design cuts against a naive reading of "open" as "unmoderated." Every entry passes through automated schema validation and then a named human reviewer before publication. This is a deliberate rejection of a fully automated or fully crowdsourced alternative, and the rejection encodes a specific epistemological claim: that trust is not a property that emerges automatically from openness, but something that has to be built through an identifiable, accountable act of judgment. An unmoderated negative-results dump would be maximally open and minimally trustworthy — indistinguishable, at the point of use, from noise. The registry bets that researchers will only actually use a source of negative evidence if someone has taken responsibility for having looked at each entry, which is a bet against the more fashionable assumption that scale and openness alone produce reliable knowledge.

## 5. The Curator as Epistemic Agent

It is worth dwelling on what the curator actually does, because the role is easy to describe administratively and hard to describe philosophically, and the philosophical description is the more accurate one. A curator reviewing a submitted negative result is not checking a fact against a ground truth, the way a fact-checker verifies a date or a quotation. There usually is no independent ground truth to check against; the submission is often the only record of the study that exists anywhere. What the curator is doing instead is closer to what a historian does with a single surviving document from a lost archive: assessing internal coherence, checking whatever external traces do exist — a source URL, a registry number, a plausible timeline — and making a judgment about whether the account holds together well enough to be worth preserving as a lead for others to follow up.

This is a genuinely different epistemic act from peer review, and the registry's own documentation is careful not to claim the two are equivalent. Peer review, at its best, subjects a completed argument to adversarial scrutiny by people qualified to find its weaknesses. Curation, as practiced here, is closer to an archivist's judgment: not "is this claim proven," but "is this claim plausible enough, sourced enough, and free enough of harm, to be worth keeping in a form someone else might find useful." The distinction matters because conflating the two would overstate what a curated entry is worth, and the entire credibility of the project depends on not overstating that.

There is a risk worth naming directly rather than around: a single curator, or even a small rotating group, becomes a chokepoint of judgment about which negative results count as real. This is precisely the kind of gatekeeping the registry exists to work around when the gatekeepers are journal editors selecting for novelty. Replacing one gatekeeper with another does not automatically improve matters unless the new gatekeeper is answerable in ways the old one was not. The registry's response — governance documentation that specifies conflict-of-interest disclosure, a public and auditable review history through ordinary version control, and an explicit, stated intention to move from a single maintainer toward a small, domain-representative group as the dataset grows — is a partial answer, not a solved problem. Whether it is a sufficient answer depends on whether the transition it promises actually happens, which is not something documentation can guarantee by itself. A thesis that pretended otherwise would not be taking the project seriously.

## 6. Limits, Risks, and the Politics of a Negative Canon

Any project that builds a canon — and a curated registry is a canon, however modest — has to reckon with what canon-formation does even when it is well intentioned. Three risks deserve direct treatment.

The first is coverage bias reproducing itself in a new form. A registry that grows primarily through voluntary submission from researchers already aware of it, and through mining public databases that are themselves disproportionately English-language and disproportionately populated by well-resourced institutions, risks building an archive of negative results that mirrors the same geographic and institutional skew as the positive-result literature it was built to counterbalance. An archive of missing evidence that is itself missing evidence from the parts of the world least able to publish through conventional channels would have repeated the original injustice in a new architecture, and would be, in an important sense, a failure disguised as a solution.

The second is the temptation to overstate what a negative entry proves. A single documented negative result is a data point, not a verdict. A field that begins treating "it's in the registry" as equivalent to "it's settled" has made the same mistake the positive-result literature already makes with a single significant p-value — mistaking one observation for a closed question. The registry's own framing, in describing entries as sourced leads rather than peer-reviewed conclusions, is an attempt to preempt this, but framing alone does not control how a resource gets used once it exists. Norms of citation and interpretation have to be actively maintained by the community that uses the archive, not assumed to follow automatically from a disclaimer in a governance document.

The third risk is closer to home and less often discussed: an infrastructure built around minimizing friction for disclosure can, without anyone intending it, minimize scrutiny along with friction. The same low barrier that lets an independent researcher document a genuine negative finding in minutes also lowers the barrier for a poorly designed study, an underpowered pilot mistaken for a real test of a hypothesis, or a result that looks negative only because the study was never capable of detecting the effect it claims to rule out. A registry optimized purely for volume would drown its signal in exactly this kind of noise. The curation layer exists to manage this risk, but managing it perfectly, at scale, without reintroducing the friction the whole system was built to eliminate, is an unsolved design tension rather than a settled achievement — and a thesis is a more honest document than a product page precisely because it can say so.

None of these risks are arguments against the registry. They are the ordinary costs of building any institution that decides what gets remembered, and the alternative — no institution at all, and therefore no memory of negative results whatsoever — is demonstrably worse, by exactly the empirical record this thesis opened with. The point of naming the risks is not to undermine the project but to insist that it be evaluated as the genuine institutional intervention it is, with the same scrutiny any institution deserves, rather than as a piece of software that either works or does not.

## 7. Conclusion

The argument of this thesis has been narrower than its title might suggest. I have not tried to show that dark data is a large problem in biomedicine — that case is made empirically, at length, elsewhere, and does not need philosophical reinforcement. I have tried to show something more specific: that a registry built to hold negative results is not merely a convenient database, but a concrete answer to a question philosophy of science raised and never institutionally resolved — what happens to knowledge that fails to confirm, and who is responsible for its disappearance.

The honest answer, before infrastructure like this existed, was: no one is responsible, because no one was ever assigned the job. Journals were assigned the job of publishing interesting results. Funders were assigned the job of funding promising ones. Universities were assigned the job of producing researchers who could get both. At no point in that chain does anyone hold the specific responsibility of making sure a negative finding survives past the researcher who produced it. A registry that takes on that responsibility explicitly — imperfectly, at small scale, with a single curator and seven illustrative entries at the time of this writing — has not solved the file-drawer problem. It has done something more modest and, I would argue, more important: it has made the absence of such a responsibility no longer the default. Someone, now, is the custodian of what did not work. That is a small institutional fact. It is also, on the account this thesis has tried to give, the actual philosophical content of the project, underneath the schemas and the workflows: a decision, finally made concrete, that silence in science is not a natural condition to be tolerated but a produced one, and that what is produced can also be undone.
